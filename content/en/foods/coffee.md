# Coffee

Coffee and coffee grounds are dangerous to pets because of their caffeine content. Dogs may be attracted to sweetened coffee drinks, while cats can be exposed through spilled espresso or used grounds. Caffeine toxicity progresses quickly and can cause hyperactivity, vomiting, tremors, seizures, and abnormal heart rhythms. Emergency treatment may include IV fluids and medications to control heart rate and seizures.

## Why Pets Cannot Handle Caffeine

Humans drink coffee to feel alert, but pets process caffeine very differently. A small dog or cat has a much lower tolerance, and the stimulant effects are stronger and longer-lasting. What feels like a mild buzz to a person can become a medical emergency for a pet.

## How Caffeine Affects the Body

Caffeine stimulates the central nervous system and cardiovascular system. Affected pets become restless and pant excessively. Their heart rate increases and may become irregular. Vomiting and diarrhea are common as the body tries to rid itself of the toxin. In severe cases, muscle tremors, seizures, and collapse occur.

## Brewed, Espresso, and Grounds

The danger increases with concentration. A few laps of diluted coffee may cause mild upset in a large dog, but espresso contains far more caffeine per ounce. Coffee grounds are the most hazardous because they are highly concentrated and may be eaten in large amounts from trash cans or compost bins.

## Hidden Caffeine Sources

Caffeine is not limited to coffee. Tea, energy drinks, soda, chocolate, diet pills, and some pain relievers also contain caffeine. Coffee-flavored ice cream, candy, and baked goods can combine caffeine with chocolate or xylitol, creating multiple hazards at once.

## Cats Are Especially Sensitive

Cats are small and lack the enzymes to break down caffeine efficiently. A small spill or a few licks from a cup can cause significant symptoms. Because cats often hide signs of illness, caffeine toxicity may progress before owners notice a problem.

## What Vets Can Do

There is no specific antidote, but supportive care is effective. The vet may induce vomiting if the ingestion was recent and give activated charcoal to limit absorption. Intravenous fluids help flush caffeine from the body, and medications can control seizures, tremors, and abnormal heart rhythms. Most pets recover with prompt care.

## Safer Drink Choices

The safest drink for pets is fresh water. For a special treat, offer a small amount of plain, low-sodium broth that does not contain onion or garlic. Avoid all caffeinated beverages and flavored drinks, including those marketed as "natural" or "decaf."
