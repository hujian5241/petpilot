# Cabbage

Cabbage is safe for dogs in small portions but is well known for causing gas. Lightly cooking cabbage makes it easier to digest than raw. Avoid coleslaw, sauerkraut, and cooked cabbage containing bacon, garlic, onions, or salt.

Cats should receive only a tiny amount. If your pet develops bloating or diarrhea, choose a different vegetable. Cabbage should not be a dietary staple.

## Why Cabbage Matters for Pets

Cabbage often shows up in home-cooked meals and vegetable scraps, so it is natural to wonder whether it is safe for dogs and cats. The good news is that plain, cooked cabbage is not toxic to either species. The challenge is that it can cause uncomfortable digestive symptoms, especially when fed raw or in large quantities. Understanding the risks helps you decide when cabbage is an acceptable treat and when a different vegetable is a better choice.

## How Cabbage Affects Dogs and Cats

Cabbage contains fiber and naturally occurring compounds that can ferment in the gut. In dogs, this fermentation commonly leads to gas, bloating, and sometimes diarrhea. Cooking the cabbage softens the fibers and may reduce these effects. Cats are less equipped to digest large amounts of plant matter, so even a small portion can cause softer stools or stomach discomfort. Long-term feeding of large amounts may also affect thyroid function because cabbage contains goitrogens.

## Common Scenarios

A dog may steal a piece of plain steamed cabbage from a plate, which is usually harmless. A bigger concern is coleslaw or sauerkraut, where salt, sugar, vinegar, garlic, or onions can cause additional problems. Some owners add raw cabbage to homemade dog food for bulk, but this often backfires with excessive gas. Cats that investigate a tiny cooked piece are unlikely to be harmed, but cabbage should never replace meat in their diet.

## What to Watch For

After your pet eats cabbage, watch for signs of digestive upset such as increased flatulence, abdominal bloating, soft stools, or diarrhea. If vomiting occurs or diarrhea lasts more than a day, contact your veterinarian. Pets with thyroid conditions, inflammatory bowel disease, or sensitive stomachs should avoid cabbage or only eat it under veterinary guidance.

## Safer Alternatives

If your dog enjoys vegetables, green beans, carrots, and zucchini are usually gentler on the stomach and lower in gas-producing compounds. For cats, small pieces of plain cooked meat or a commercial cat treat are more appropriate than vegetables. When you want to add fiber or bulk to a dog's meal, pumpkin or cooked green beans are often better tolerated than cabbage.
