# Carrots

Carrots are a safe, crunchy vegetable for dogs and cats. They are low in calories and high in fiber and beta-carotene. Many dogs enjoy raw carrots as a chew treat, which can help scrape plaque from teeth. For cats, carrots should be offered only as an occasional tiny treat since they do not require vegetables in their diet.

Cooking carrots makes them easier to digest and is a good option for older pets or those with dental issues. Avoid canned carrots with added salt or sugar. Always cut carrots into appropriately sized pieces to reduce choking risk, especially for small dogs and puppies.

## Why Carrots Matter for Pets

Carrots are one of the most commonly recommended vegetables for dogs because they are inexpensive, easy to prepare, and low in calories. They can serve as a healthy alternative to high-calorie commercial treats, especially for pets watching their weight. Their natural crunch also appeals to dogs who enjoy chewing.

## How Carrots Affect Dogs and Cats

Carrots contain beta-carotene, which the body converts into vitamin A to support eye health and immune function. The fiber in carrots can help regulate digestion, though too much fiber may cause loose stool. Dogs generally digest cooked carrots more easily than raw ones, while cats may not digest vegetables as efficiently and need only very small amounts.

## Common Scenarios

- **Raw carrot sticks:** A popular chew treat for dogs when cut to size.
- **Cooked carrots:** Easier to digest for seniors or pets with dental problems.
- **Shredded carrots:** A small meal topper for dogs or cats.
- **Canned carrots:** Often contain added salt; avoid for pets.
- **Large whole carrots:** Can be a choking risk for small dogs and puppies.

## What to Watch For

Watch for choking, especially with large or whole carrots. Some pets may develop orange-tinged stool after eating carrots, which is usually harmless. Diarrhea or gas may occur if too many carrots are fed. If your pet chokes or shows persistent digestive symptoms, contact your vet.

## Safer Alternatives

If your pet does not tolerate carrots, consider green beans, cucumber slices, or small pieces of cooked sweet potato. These options also provide vitamins and fiber with low calorie content.
