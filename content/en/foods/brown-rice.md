# Brown Rice

Brown rice is a nutritious whole grain that supplies fiber, B vitamins, and minerals. Many dogs digest it well when it is thoroughly cooked and served in moderation. Its higher fiber content can be helpful for some dogs but may cause gas or loose stool in others.

Cats can eat brown rice occasionally, but it is not a natural part of their diet and should be limited. Never feed seasoned rice dishes, fried rice, or rice pilaf to pets, as these often contain onion, garlic, salt, and fats that are harmful.

## Why Brown Rice Matters for Pets

Pet owners often choose brown rice over white rice because it feels more natural and nutrient-dense. The bran layer adds fiber, which can help some dogs maintain regular bowel movements. For healthy adult dogs, brown rice can be a reasonable occasional carbohydrate source. The challenge is that not every dog tolerates the extra fiber well, especially those with sensitive stomachs or inflammatory bowel conditions.

## How Brown Rice Affects Dogs and Cats

Because brown rice still has its bran layer, it digests more slowly than white rice. This slower digestion can help some pets feel full longer, but it can also lead to fermentation in the gut. Fermentation produces gas and, in some cases, loose stools. Cooking the rice until very soft improves digestibility. Cats, being obligate carnivores, gain little from brown rice and may find it harder to digest than dogs do.

## Common Scenarios

- **Homemade meals.** Some owners add brown rice to balance a home-cooked diet. This should only be done with veterinary guidance to ensure nutritional completeness.
- **Fiber boost.** A small amount of brown rice may help a dog with mild constipation, but too much can cause the opposite problem.
- **Sensitive stomach.** A dog with a history of digestive issues may do better on white rice than brown rice.
- **Cat tasting.** A cat may sniff or lick a tiny bit of brown rice, but it should not become a regular offering.
- **Weight management.** Although brown rice is whole grain, it still adds calories and should be measured carefully.
- **IBD flare.** During a digestive flare, the fiber in brown rice may worsen diarrhea and discomfort.

## What to Watch For

Signs that brown rice does not agree with your pet include increased gas, bloating, soft stool, or diarrhea. If these occur, switch to white rice or remove grains from the diet temporarily. For cats, make sure rice is not replacing meat-based food. Any ongoing digestive symptoms should be discussed with your veterinarian to rule out underlying conditions.

## Safer Alternatives

If brown rice causes problems, white rice is usually gentler on the stomach. Plain oatmeal or quinoa can be alternatives for some dogs, but they also need to be introduced slowly. For cats, the best approach is to skip grains entirely and focus on high-quality animal protein. Vegetables like cooked pumpkin or green beans can provide fiber with fewer digestive risks for dogs.
