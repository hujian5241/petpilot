# Oranges

Orange flesh is generally safe in tiny amounts, but the oils in the peel and high acidity can cause stomach upset.

## Why Oranges Matter for Pets

Oranges are a staple fruit in many households, so it is common for pets to encounter them. While the flesh is not poisonous, oranges are not an ideal treat for dogs or cats. The acidity can irritate the stomach, the peel and pith can cause digestive upset, and the sugar adds unnecessary calories.

## How Oranges Affect Dogs and Cats

A small segment of orange flesh may be tolerated by some dogs, though many dogs dislike the tart flavor. The citric acid and natural sugars can cause vomiting or diarrhea if too much is eaten. Cats generally avoid oranges because the strong citrus smell is unpleasant to them. The peel and white pith are the most irritating parts and should never be fed.

## Common Scenarios

A dog might lick a dropped piece of orange flesh, which is usually harmless in tiny amounts. A bigger concern is a pet chewing on an orange peel or eating multiple segments. Some owners consider orange juice, but the concentrated sugar and acidity make it a poor choice. Cats are unlikely to eat oranges, but any citrus exposure should be monitored.

## What to Watch For

Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, drooling, or loss of appetite after citrus exposure. If your pet ate a large amount of peel or shows persistent symptoms, contact your veterinarian. Pets with diabetes, obesity, dental disease, or sensitive stomachs should avoid oranges or have them only on veterinary advice.

## Safer Alternatives

Blueberries, apple slices, and small pieces of banana are usually better fruit choices for dogs. For cats, meat-based treats are ideal. If you want to offer something juicy and hydrating, small pieces of seedless watermelon or cucumber slices are often gentler on the stomach than oranges.
