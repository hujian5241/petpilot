# Rolled Oats

Rolled oats are whole-grain oats that have been steamed and flattened. They are safe for dogs once cooked into oatmeal or baked into simple, pet-safe treats. Feeding a large amount of dry rolled oats can lead to bloating as the oats absorb moisture in the stomach.

Cats do not need oats in their diet, but a small amount of cooked oatmeal is generally safe. Avoid instant oatmeal packets, which often contain added sugar, salt, and artificial flavors.

## Why Rolled Oats Matter for Pets

Oats have a reputation as a heart-healthy human food, and that image carries over to pets. Many homemade treat recipes use oatmeal because it is affordable, widely available, and easy to digest when cooked. The main risk comes from feeding oats dry or choosing flavored versions loaded with sugar and salt. Plain cooked oatmeal can be a useful bland food, but it should never become a dietary staple.

## How Rolled Oats Affect Dogs and Cats

Cooked rolled oats provide soluble fiber that can help firm loose stools in some dogs. They also supply small amounts of iron, B vitamins, and manganese. Because oats are calorie-dense, they add up quickly. Cats lack the enzymes to efficiently process large amounts of grain, so a spoonful is plenty. Raw or dry oats pose the greatest concern because they expand in the digestive tract and may cause painful bloating.

## Common Scenarios

- Preparing a bland meal after a dog has had mild diarrhea
- Baking peanut butter oatmeal dog treats
- Discovering your cat licked leftover breakfast oatmeal
- Spilling a container of dry oats and finding your pet eating them
- Comparing steel-cut oats, rolled oats, and instant oats for pets
- Adding oats to a homemade diet without veterinary guidance

## What to Watch For

After feeding cooked oats, watch for gas, bloating, or soft stool. These signs usually mean the portion was too large or the oats were not cooked enough. If your pet consumed a large amount of dry oats, offer water and monitor for distended abdomen, repeated vomiting, or signs of pain. Contact your veterinarian if these symptoms occur.

## Safer Alternatives

Plain white rice or quinoa can serve a similar bland-carbohydrate role. For cats, skip grains entirely and focus on high-quality meat-based treats. If fiber is the goal, small amounts of cooked pumpkin or steamed green beans may be more appropriate and lower in calories.
