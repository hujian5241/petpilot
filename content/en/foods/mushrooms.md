# Mushrooms

While common store-bought mushrooms are not known to be toxic to pets, mushrooms are generally best avoided because many wild species are highly poisonous and difficult to identify. Some wild mushrooms can cause liver failure, seizures, or death.

If your pet eats a mushroom in the yard, assume it is dangerous until proven otherwise. Contact a veterinarian or poison control immediately and bring a sample or clear photo for identification. Do not rely on internet images for mushroom identification.

## Why Mushrooms Matter for Pets

Mushrooms deserve extra attention because the difference between a safe and a deadly species is not obvious. Even experienced foragers can misidentify wild mushrooms, so pet owners should treat all yard mushrooms as potentially dangerous. Quick action can save a pet's life.

## How Mushrooms Affect Dogs and Cats

Toxic mushroom compounds can damage the liver, kidneys, or nervous system depending on the species. Symptoms may include vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, seizures, or jaundice. Some toxins act quickly, while others cause delayed organ failure, making early veterinary care essential.

## Common Scenarios

- A dog eats a mushroom during a backyard play session.
- Mushrooms sprout after rain and a curious pet investigates.
- A pet licks leftover cooked mushrooms from a plate.
- A cat finds a small mushroom growing in a houseplant.

## What to Watch For

Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, excessive drooling, weakness, tremors, seizures, or yellowing of the skin and eyes. Even if symptoms seem mild at first, they can worsen rapidly. Contact a vet or poison control immediately if you suspect mushroom ingestion.

## Safer Alternatives

Carrots, green beans, or small pieces of cooked sweet potato are safer vegetable options. They provide nutrition without the identification risks that mushrooms carry.
