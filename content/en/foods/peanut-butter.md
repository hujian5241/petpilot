# Peanut Butter

Plain peanut butter without xylitol is a popular and generally safe treat for dogs. It can be used for hiding pills or as a training reward. Always read the label carefully for xylitol.

Cats can have a very small taste, but peanut butter is high in fat and not a natural part of their diet.

## Why Peanut Butter Matters for Pets

Peanut butter is a staple treat in many households because it is affordable, easy to portion, and highly motivating for dogs. It can turn a boring Kong toy into a long-lasting puzzle and make medication time much easier. The danger lies in assuming all peanut butter is the same. Some brands, especially sugar-free and specialty versions, contain xylitol, which is extremely toxic to dogs. A quick label check before every jar is a simple habit that can save a life.

## How Peanut Butter Affects Dogs and Cats

The high fat and protein content make peanut butter satisfying and energy-dense. For healthy dogs, an occasional lick is usually fine. For cats, the fat content is harder to justify nutritionally because they need animal-based proteins and fats. The sticky texture can also be a choking risk if too much is given at once. Peanut butter should be treated as a high-value reward, not a meal component.

## Common Scenarios

- **Kong stuffing.** A thin smear inside a toy can keep a dog occupied. Avoid overfilling because calories add up.
- **Pill camouflage.** A pea-sized amount can wrap a pill, but ask your vet if this is suitable for the medication.
- **Training reward.** Dogs will work hard for peanut butter, making it useful for recall or grooming training.
- **Sugar-free swap.** A well-meaning owner buys a healthier-looking sugar-free brand that contains xylitol.
- **Sharing with a cat.** Most cats do not care for peanut butter, and the high fat load is unnecessary.
- **Pancreatitis risk.** A pet with a history of pancreatitis gets a large spoonful and develops vomiting and lethargy a day later.

## What to Watch For

If your dog ate peanut butter containing xylitol, watch for vomiting, weakness, staggering, tremors, seizures, or collapse. These signs can appear quickly and require emergency veterinary care. For regular peanut butter, watch for diarrhea, vomiting, or decreased appetite, especially in pets with sensitive stomachs. Weight gain is another sign that portions are too large.

## Safer Alternatives

For a lower-calorie treat, try plain canned pumpkin, small pieces of carrot, or apple slices. Plain yogurt can also work for pill hiding if your pet tolerates dairy. If your dog loves the challenge of a filled toy, consider wet dog food frozen inside a Kong instead of peanut butter. These swaps provide enrichment without the concentrated fat and calorie load.
