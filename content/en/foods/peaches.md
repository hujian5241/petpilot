# Peaches

Peach flesh is generally safe for dogs in small amounts, but the pit contains cyanide and poses a choking hazard.

## Why Peaches Matter for Pets

Peaches are a juicy, fragrant fruit that many dogs will happily accept. The flesh is not toxic in small portions, but the pit is a serious hazard. Peach pits contain compounds that can release cyanide when chewed, and their size makes them likely to cause choking or intestinal blockage. Safe preparation is the key to sharing peaches with pets.

## How Peaches Affect Dogs and Cats

Small pieces of ripe peach flesh are soft and easy for most dogs to chew. The natural sugar can cause an upset stomach or diarrhea if too much is given. The real danger lies in the pit, stem, and leaves, all of which contain cyanide-releasing compounds. Cats are less likely to eat peaches, but they face the same risks if they chew on the pit or foliage.

## Common Scenarios

A typical safe scenario is a dog receiving one or two small slices of peeled, pitted peach flesh. A dangerous scenario is a dog grabbing a whole peach from a fruit bowl or eating fallen peaches from a tree. Backyard peach trees can be especially risky because dogs may chew on pits, stems, or leaves. Canned peaches are also problematic because of added sugar.

## What to Watch For

If your pet ate peach flesh, watch for vomiting or diarrhea. If the pit was chewed or swallowed, contact your veterinarian immediately even if your pet seems fine. Warning signs of blockage include repeated vomiting, loss of appetite, straining to defecate, and abdominal pain. Signs of cyanide exposure may include difficulty breathing, drooling, and weakness.

## Safer Alternatives

Apple slices without seeds and blueberries are safer fruit options for dogs. For cats, meat-based treats are more appropriate than fruit. If your dog enjoys soft, sweet treats, small pieces of banana or seedless watermelon can be lower-risk choices when given in moderation.
