# Xylitol

Xylitol is a sugar substitute found in sugar-free gum, candy, baked goods, and some peanut butters. In dogs, it can trigger a rapid insulin release, causing severe hypoglycemia and possible liver failure.

## A Hidden Toxin in Everyday Products

Xylitol is popular in sugar-free and low-calorie products because it tastes sweet but has fewer calories than sugar. For pets, however, it is a silent hazard. It appears in items that owners may not think of as dangerous, such as peanut butter, toothpaste, and chewable vitamins. Because many people use peanut butter as a treat or to hide medication, xylitol-containing versions are a particularly common source of poisoning.

## Why Dogs Are So Vulnerable

In dogs, xylitol is absorbed quickly and causes the pancreas to release a large amount of insulin. This insulin rush drives blood sugar down to dangerous levels, often within an hour. The brain relies heavily on glucose, so low blood sugar leads quickly to weakness, confusion, seizures, and collapse. Even if a dog survives the hypoglycemic crisis, liver damage can develop days later.

## The Two Phases of Poisoning

The first phase is hypoglycemia. Signs include vomiting, stumbling, trembling, and lethargy. If treated promptly with intravenous glucose and supportive care, many dogs recover. The second phase is liver injury, which may not show symptoms for one to three days. Blood tests can reveal rising liver enzymes, jaundice, and clotting problems. In severe cases, liver failure is fatal.

## How Much Is Too Much

Toxicity depends on the product's xylitol concentration and the pet's weight. Some sugar-free gums contain 0.3 to 1 gram of xylitol per piece. For a 10-pound dog, just one piece can be enough to cause hypoglycemia, and a few pieces can threaten the liver. Because labels do not always list the exact amount, any ingestion should be treated as an emergency.

## Products That May Contain Xylitol

Besides gum and candy, xylitol is found in sugar-free baked goods, protein bars, pudding, ice cream, flavored water, nasal sprays, sleep aids, and melatonin gummies. Some brands of peanut butter and nut butters add it as a sweetener. Any product labeled "sugar-free," "no sugar added," or "diabetic-friendly" should be checked carefully before sharing with pets.

## What to Expect at the Vet

If the ingestion was recent, the vet may induce vomiting and give activated charcoal. Blood glucose is monitored closely, and dextrose is given if needed. Intravenous fluids, liver protectants, and clotting support may be required. Dogs with severe liver involvement may need several days of hospitalization.

## Prevention Tips

Store purses, backpacks, and coats containing gum out of reach. Keep toothpaste and medications in closed cabinets. Read labels on peanut butter and other shared foods. Teach children not to offer candy or gum to pets, and dispose of sugar-free products in a secure trash can.
