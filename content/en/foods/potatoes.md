# Potatoes

Plain cooked white potatoes are safe for dogs in moderation. They are starchy and high in carbohydrates, so they should be limited for overweight pets. Raw potatoes and any green parts of the potato contain solanine, which is toxic.

Cats can have a small bite of plain cooked potato. Avoid mashed potatoes with butter, cream, garlic, salt, or gravy. Never let pets chew on potato plants or sprouts.

## Why Potatoes Matter for Pets

Potatoes are a staple food in many kitchens, making them easy for pets to encounter. Whether raw, cooked, green, or processed, the form matters greatly. Understanding the difference between safe cooked potato and dangerous raw or green potato can help prevent accidental poisoning and unnecessary vet visits.

## How Potatoes Affect Dogs and Cats

Small amounts of plain cooked potato are generally safe for dogs and can provide a source of carbohydrates. Raw potatoes, green potatoes, and potato sprouts contain solanine, a compound that can cause vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, and neurological signs. Cats have little need for starchy vegetables and should only receive tiny portions, if any.

## Common Scenarios

- **Plain boiled or baked potato:** A small bite is usually safe.
- **Mashed potatoes with butter or gravy:** Too fatty and salty; avoid.
- **French fries:** High in fat and salt; not suitable for pets.
- **Raw potato:** Contact your vet due to solanine risk.
- **Green or sprouted potato:** More toxic; seek veterinary advice.

## What to Watch For

After ingestion of raw or green potato, watch for vomiting, diarrhea, drooling, weakness, confusion, or dilated pupils. These signs may indicate solanine toxicity and require veterinary care. Cooked potato is less risky but may cause gas or diarrhea in sensitive pets.

## Safer Alternatives

For a safer starchy vegetable, consider cooked plain sweet potato, pumpkin, or carrots. These options often provide more nutrients and fiber with lower solanine risk. Always serve them plain and in small portions.
