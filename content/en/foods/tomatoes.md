# Tomatoes

Ripe tomato flesh is generally considered safe for dogs in small amounts. The plant, stems, leaves, and unripe fruit are more concerning.

Tomatoes belong to the nightshade family, and their green parts contain compounds that can be toxic to pets. Most pets will not eat enough to cause serious harm, but it is still wise to keep tomato plants and unripe fruit out of reach.

## Why Tomatoes Matter for Pets

Tomatoes are everywhere in human food, from fresh salads to pasta sauces. Pets may encounter cherry tomatoes, garden plants, or dropped sauce. While ripe flesh is usually safe, the rest of the plant can cause problems. Being able to tell the difference helps owners react appropriately.

## How Tomatoes Affect Dogs and Cats

Ripe red tomato flesh contains very low levels of tomatine and is unlikely to cause issues in small amounts. In contrast, tomato stems, leaves, and green fruit contain higher concentrations of solanine and tomatine, which can lead to gastrointestinal upset, lethargy, and confusion. Cats generally have no interest in tomatoes and should not be encouraged to eat them.

## Common Scenarios

- **A small piece of ripe tomato:** Usually safe for dogs.
- **Tomato plant or leaves:** Potentially toxic; contact your vet.
- **Green or unripe tomatoes:** Higher toxin levels; avoid.
- **Tomato sauce or ketchup:** Contains unsafe additives; do not share.
- **A cat near a tomato plant:** Supervise outdoor access to gardens.

## What to Watch For

Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, drooling, weakness, dilated pupils, or confusion after tomato plant exposure. If your pet ate stems, leaves, or a large amount of green tomato, contact your vet or a poison control center. Mild upset from ripe flesh usually resolves on its own.

## Safer Alternatives

For a safe vegetable treat, try carrots, cucumber slices, or small pieces of cooked green beans. These options are less likely to cause digestive upset and do not carry the same plant-toxin concerns as tomatoes.
