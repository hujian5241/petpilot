# Pork

Plain, cooked pork can be a tasty protein option for pets, but it must be cooked thoroughly to eliminate the risk of trichinella and other parasites. Lean cuts such as tenderloin are best.

Avoid raw pork and processed pork products like bacon and ham, which are high in salt and fat. Never feed cooked bones.

## Why Pork Matters for Pets

Pork appears in many homes as roasts, chops, bacon, and sausage. Pets may be drawn to the smell and flavor, but not all forms are safe. Feeding fatty, seasoned, or undercooked pork can lead to vomiting, diarrhea, pancreatitis, or parasite infections. Understanding how to prepare pork correctly helps pet owners share an occasional safe bite without putting their dog or cat at risk.

## How Pork Affects Dogs and Cats

When fully cooked and served plain, lean pork is generally well tolerated by both dogs and cats. The protein is digestible and can be offered as a small treat. However, raw or undercooked pork may carry trichinella parasites and harmful bacteria. Processed pork products often contain high levels of sodium and fat, which can irritate the stomach and may trigger pancreatitis in susceptible pets.

## Common Scenarios

- **Cooked plain pork:** A small bite of unseasoned, thoroughly cooked pork is usually fine.
- **Bacon or sausage:** These are too salty and fatty and should be avoided.
- **Raw pork:** Contact your vet, as parasites and bacteria are a concern.
- **Cooked bones:** Can splinter and cause choking or internal injury; never feed them.
- **Ham or glazed pork:** Often high in sugar, salt, and seasonings; not suitable for pets.

## What to Watch For

Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, loss of appetite, lethargy, or abdominal pain. If your pet ate raw or undercooked pork, your vet may recommend parasite testing or monitoring. Signs of pancreatitis, such as repeated vomiting or a hunched posture, require prompt veterinary care.

## Safer Alternatives

If you want a safer protein treat, consider plain cooked chicken breast, turkey, or lean beef. These options are lower in fat and do not carry the same parasite risk as pork. Always serve them unseasoned and in small portions.
