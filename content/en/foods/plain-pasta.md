# Plain Pasta

Plain, cooked pasta is safe for dogs as an occasional treat. It is bland and easy to chew, making it a common vehicle for hiding medications. Pasta should make up only a small fraction of a dog's diet to avoid excess carbohydrates and weight gain.

Cats can have a small piece of plain pasta on rare occasions. Tomato sauce, Alfredo, pesto, and garlic bread are not safe for pets. Whole-wheat or gluten-free pasta is also safe as long as it is plain and cooked.

## Why Plain Pasta Matters for Pets

Pasta is one of the most common leftovers in homes, and dogs are quick to hover around a dropped noodle. The good news is that a small amount of plain cooked pasta is generally safe. The bad news is that most pasta dishes come with ingredients that are not pet-friendly. Keeping pasta plain and portioned keeps it harmless.

## How Plain Pasta Affects Dogs and Cats

Cooked pasta is mostly starch and water. It is easy to digest in small amounts but offers little protein, vitamins, or minerals. For dogs, it can serve as a bland vehicle for pills. For cats, who need animal-based protein, pasta is essentially empty calories. The biggest risks come from garlic, onions, salt, cheese, cream, and certain herbs often found in sauces.

## Common Scenarios

- Wrapping a pill in a small piece of plain cooked pasta
- Cleaning up a plate of leftover spaghetti Bolognese
- Sharing buttered noodles with your dog
- Feeding pasta salad that contains onion or dressing
- Dropping a single plain noodle while cooking
- Trying gluten-free pasta for a pet with a sensitive stomach

## What to Watch For

Most plain pasta causes no problems. If the pasta contained sauce or seasoning, watch for vomiting, diarrhea, excessive thirst, or lethargy. Pets with wheat allergies may develop itchy skin, ear infections, or chronic digestive issues. If your pet has a known allergy or ate a large amount of seasoned pasta, contact your veterinarian.

## Safer Alternatives

Plain white rice, plain bread, or cooked quinoa are similarly bland options. For hiding medication, a small piece of cooked chicken or a vet-approved pill pocket is often better. If your pet needs carbohydrates for energy, choose a balanced commercial diet formulated for their species and life stage.
