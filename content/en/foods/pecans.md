# Pecans

Pecans are not as toxic as macadamia nuts, but they are high in fat and can cause pancreatitis or gastrointestinal upset. Moldy pecans can also contain tremorgenic mycotoxins.

## Why Pecans Matter for Pets

Pecans are a common household nut, especially during holidays and baking seasons. Their rich, buttery flavor makes them attractive to dogs, and their small size means they can be swallowed quickly. Because pecans are often stored in bulk, they can also become moldy without obvious signs. Pet owners should be cautious about leaving pecans, pecan-containing foods, or shells within reach.

## How Pecans Affects Dogs and Cats

The main concern with pecans is their very high fat content. In dogs, a sudden fatty meal can irritate the stomach and may lead to pancreatitis, a painful inflammation of the pancreas that often requires veterinary care. Moldy pecans may contain tremorgenic mycotoxins, which can cause tremors, weakness, or seizures in severe cases.

Cats lack the digestive capacity to handle large amounts of fat from plant sources. While a tiny taste is unlikely to be dangerous, pecans offer no nutritional benefit and should be avoided as a regular treat.

## Common Scenarios

- A dog raids a bowl of whole or shelled pecans left on a table.
- A pet licks pecan pie filling, which contains sugar, spices, and sometimes alcohol.
- A dog chews on discarded pecan shells in the trash.
- A cat shows interest in buttered or candied pecan pieces.

## What to Watch For

Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, loss of appetite, lethargy, or abdominal pain. Contact your veterinarian promptly if your pet ate a large amount, if shells were swallowed, or if you notice tremors, stumbling, or seizures.

## Safer Alternatives

Carrot sticks, apple slices without seeds, and blueberries are lower-risk treats that provide crunch or sweetness without the fat and mold concerns of pecans. Stick to these options for everyday rewards.
