# Zucchini

Zucchini is a mild, easy-to-digest vegetable that is safe for most dogs. It is low in calories and can be added to meals as a filler for overweight pets. Raw zucchini is safe, but cooking it makes it softer and easier to digest.

Cats can have a small amount of plain cooked zucchini. Avoid zucchini bread or dishes containing garlic, onions, or cheese. Always introduce new vegetables gradually to watch for digestive upset.

## Why Zucchini Matters for Pets

Zucchini is one of the safest vegetables to share with dogs. Its mild flavor and soft texture when cooked make it appealing to many pets, and its low calorie count makes it useful for weight management. Unlike some vegetables that cause gas or digestive distress, zucchini is usually well tolerated in reasonable amounts.

## How Zucchini Affects Dogs and Cats

The high water content in zucchini makes it hydrating and filling without contributing many calories. The fiber supports regular bowel movements, though too much can lead to loose stools. Cooking breaks down the cell walls and makes the nutrients more accessible, which is why cooked zucchini is often preferred for cats and dogs with sensitive stomachs.

## Common Scenarios

Many owners add steamed zucchini to a dog's bowl as a low-calorie filler. Raw zucchini slices can also work as a crunchy treat for dogs that enjoy them. Problems usually arise when zucchini is part of a prepared dish containing garlic, onions, cheese, or excessive oil. Zucchini bread is especially risky because it may contain raisins, xylitol, or chocolate.

## What to Watch For

Zucchini rarely causes problems, but any new food can trigger digestive upset. Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, or loss of appetite after the first few servings. If symptoms persist beyond a day, contact your veterinarian. Pets with diabetes or obesity can usually have zucchini, but it should be counted toward their daily calorie intake.

## Safer Alternatives

Cucumber, green beans, and carrots are similar low-calorie options for dogs. For cats, small pieces of plain cooked meat or commercial treats are more appropriate than vegetables. If you are trying to help a dog lose weight, ask your veterinarian about safe portion sizes and whether replacing part of the regular food with vegetables is appropriate.
