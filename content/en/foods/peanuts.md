# Peanuts

Peanuts are not truly nuts; they are legumes. Plain, unsalted peanuts are generally safe for dogs in very small amounts, but their high fat content can lead to stomach upset or pancreatitis. Salted or flavored peanuts can cause sodium problems.

Cats may tolerate a tiny taste, but peanuts offer little nutritional value and can cause digestive upset. Always avoid peanuts coated in chocolate, xylitol, or other dangerous ingredients.

## Why Peanuts Matter for Pets

Peanuts are everywhere in homes, from snack bowls to trail mix. A few dropped nuts may seem harmless, but the fat and salt add up quickly. Coated peanuts can also hide chocolate, xylitol, or garlic powder, which are dangerous for pets.

## How Peanuts Affect Dogs and Cats

Plain peanuts are not toxic, but their high fat content can cause vomiting or diarrhea. In susceptible pets, too many peanuts can trigger pancreatitis. Whole peanuts are hard to chew and may cause choking or blockage, especially in small dogs.

## Common Scenarios

- A few plain peanuts fall on the floor during snack time.
- A dog tears open a bag of salted or honey-roasted peanuts.
- Peanuts are used as an improvised training treat.
- A pet eats chocolate-covered or candy-coated peanuts.
- A puppy finds a whole peanut outside.

## What to Watch For

Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, gas, lethargy, or loss of appetite. If your pet ate chocolate-covered peanuts or shows signs of pancreatitis such as abdominal pain, contact your vet. Seek immediate help if you suspect choking or blockage.

## Safer Alternatives

Offer small pieces of carrots, apple slices, or green beans instead. These vegetables are low in fat and provide fiber. If you want to use peanut butter, choose plain, unsalted, xylitol-free peanut butter in tiny amounts.
