# Raisins

Raisins are dried grapes and carry the same kidney toxicity risk, often in a more concentrated form. Even a small handful can be life-threatening.

## Why Raisins Are Especially Dangerous

Because raisins have had most of their water removed, the toxic compounds are packed into a small, chewy form. A dog can swallow dozens of raisins in seconds without realizing the danger. For a small dog, just a few raisins can be enough to trigger kidney injury.

## Common Hiding Places

Raisins appear in oatmeal raisin cookies, raisin bread, granola bars, trail mix, cereals, fruitcake, and rice pudding. They are also used in savory dishes like pilafs and tagines. Any food containing raisins, currants, or sultanas should be kept away from pets.

## The Same Risk as Grapes

Raisins and grapes share the same unknown toxin. The same rules apply: there is no safe amount, and toxicity is unpredictable. Some dogs eat raisins without visible illness, while others develop life-threatening kidney failure. There is no way to know which dog will react.

## Why Waiting Is a Mistake

Kidney damage from raisins may not show symptoms for a day or two. By the time a dog is vomiting, lethargic, and not urinating, the kidneys may already be severely injured. Calling poison control or a vet immediately allows for early decontamination and fluid therapy, which can prevent permanent damage.

## What Vets May Do

If the raisins were eaten recently, the vet may induce vomiting and give activated charcoal. Intravenous fluids are typically given for 48 hours to support the kidneys and flush toxins. Blood and urine tests monitor kidney function. With prompt care, many dogs recover fully.

## Safer Sweet Treats

If your dog enjoys chewy or sweet treats, offer small pieces of apple, banana, blueberries, or watermelon. These fruits are safe in moderation and do not carry the kidney risk of grapes and raisins. Always remove seeds and offer them in small portions.
