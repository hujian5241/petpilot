# Sugar

Plain sugar is not toxic to pets, but it provides empty calories and can contribute to obesity, dental disease, and diabetes. Sugary foods often contain other dangerous ingredients.

Avoid giving sugar or sweets to pets. Choose natural, low-sugar treats instead.

## Why Sugar Matters for Pets

Sugar is not poisonous in the way chocolate or xylitol are, but it is still one of the most common contributors to poor pet health. Treats, baked goods, and cereals can pack in surprising amounts of sugar. Over time, this can lead to weight gain, insulin resistance, and painful dental disease.

## How Sugar Affects Dogs and Cats

When pets eat sugar, their blood glucose rises quickly. Repeated spikes can stress the pancreas and may increase the risk of diabetes. Sugar also feeds oral bacteria, leading to plaque buildup and bad breath. Some pets may vomit or have diarrhea after eating a large amount.

## Common Scenarios

- A dog steals cookies, cake, or candy from a counter.
- Sugar is added to homemade treats without realizing the risk.
- Cereal, granola, or baked goods are left within reach.
- A child shares a sweet snack with a pet.

## What to Watch For

Look for vomiting, diarrhea, restlessness, increased thirst, or lethargy. If a sugary food also contained chocolate, raisins, xylitol, or macadamia nuts, contact your vet or a poison helpline immediately. Long-term signs include weight gain and dental problems.

## Safer Alternatives

Choose low-sugar treats such as blueberries, apple slices, or carrots. These foods add fiber and vitamins without the empty calories. Always check labels for hidden sugars and artificial sweeteners.
