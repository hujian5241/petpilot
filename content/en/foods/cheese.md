# Cheese

Cheese is a popular training treat for dogs, and many dogs tolerate small amounts well. However, cheese is high in fat and sodium, and some dogs are lactose intolerant. Low-fat options like mozzarella or cottage cheese are usually better choices.

Cats may enjoy a tiny piece of cheese, but dairy is not a natural part of their diet. Avoid strongly flavored, aged, or moldy cheeses, and never feed cheese dishes that contain garlic, onions, or herbs.

## Why Cheese Matters for Pets

Cheese is everywhere in human food, which makes it easy to share with pets without much thought. It works well for hiding medication and motivating dogs during training. The problem is that many cheeses are calorie-dense and high in sodium, so frequent feeding can contribute to obesity. Some varieties also contain ingredients that are outright toxic to pets. Choosing the right type and portion matters more than most owners realize.

## How Cheese Affects Dogs and Cats

Most cheeses contain less lactose than milk because the fermentation process breaks some of it down. This is why some lactose-intolerant pets can handle cheese even if milk upsets their stomach. Still, cheese is rich in fat, which can be hard on the pancreas, especially in dogs prone to pancreatitis. Salt content can also be an issue for pets with heart or kidney disease. Cats process dairy similarly but require much smaller portions because of their size.

## Common Scenarios

- **Training rewards.** Small cubes of low-fat cheese can motivate dogs, but calories add up quickly. Break treats into tiny pieces.
- **Pill hiding.** A thin slice of cheese can wrap a pill, but check with your vet if your pet needs a low-fat diet.
- **Party and table scraps.** Rich, fatty, or seasoned cheeses from appetizers can cause vomiting or diarrhea.
- **Blue cheese or moldy cheese.** These should be avoided because they may contain substances that can be toxic to dogs.
- **Garlic-herb cheese.** Garlic and onions are dangerous for both dogs and cats, even in small amounts.
- **Pets with health conditions.** Animals with pancreatitis, obesity, or kidney disease should skip cheese unless your vet approves.

## What to Watch For

After eating cheese, watch for signs of digestive upset such as diarrhea, gas, vomiting, or a decreased appetite. If your dog shows abdominal pain, hunched posture, repeated vomiting, or lethargy after a high-fat meal, contact your veterinarian promptly because pancreatitis may be developing. Cats may show nausea by hiding, drooling, or refusing food. Any sudden change in behavior after eating a new food is worth monitoring.

## Safer Alternatives

For training, consider small pieces of carrot, apple, or plain cooked chicken breast. Plain yogurt or cottage cheese can occasionally replace cheese if your pet tolerates dairy. If you need a pill pocket, ask your veterinarian about low-calorie or veterinary-formulated options. These alternatives can provide the same motivation without the heavy fat and salt load.
