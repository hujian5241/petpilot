# Salt

Salt is an essential mineral, but pets can quickly consume too much when they eat seasoned human foods, broths, or homemade play dough. Sodium toxicity causes dehydration and can progress to tremors, seizures, and brain swelling. Treatment often requires IV fluids and careful electrolyte monitoring. Keep salty snacks such as chips, pretzels, and seasoned nuts away from pets.

## Why Salt Matters for Pets

Pets need sodium for normal body function, but the amount in a typical salty snack can be many times what they should have. Dogs and cats do not sweat the way humans do, and their kidneys can be overwhelmed by sudden high salt loads. Seasoned human foods, broths, and crafts like salt dough are common sources of dangerous overexposure.

## How Salt Affects Dogs and Cats

Excess sodium pulls water out of cells and leads to dehydration. The body tries to correct the imbalance by drinking more water, but if too much salt was eaten, the brain may swell or electrical signals may misfire. This can result in vomiting, diarrhea, tremors, weakness, and seizures.

## Common Scenarios

- A pet eats chips, pretzels, popcorn, or salted nuts left on a table.
- A dog chews a homemade salt dough ornament or play dough.
- A container of table salt spills and is licked up.
- Salty broth, soup, or gravy is shared with a pet.

## What to Watch For

Early signs include excessive thirst, frequent urination, and vomiting. More serious signs include tremors, weakness, difficulty walking, and seizures. If you suspect a large salt ingestion, contact your vet or an emergency clinic immediately.

## Safer Alternatives

Offer plain, unseasoned cooked chicken breast, brown rice, or steamed carrots. These foods are naturally low in sodium and provide real nutrition. Avoid adding salt, garlic, onion powder, or bouillon to anything your pet eats.
