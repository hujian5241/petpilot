# Onions

Onions are toxic to dogs and cats in all forms, including raw, cooked, dehydrated, and powdered. They contain compounds that can damage red blood cells, leading to hemolytic anemia. Symptoms may be delayed for several days.

## Why Every Form Matters

Many people assume that cooking destroys toxins, but onion toxicity is different. The harmful compounds in onions are stable at cooking temperatures. Whether raw, roasted, fried, dehydrated, or powdered, onions can poison pets. Powdered and dehydrated forms are especially concentrated, so a small amount can deliver a large dose.

## How Onions Damage the Blood

The toxic substances in onions attach to hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen. Once damaged, the cells rupture and are cleared from circulation. The bone marrow tries to replace them, but if destruction outpaces production, anemia develops. Pets become weak, breathe faster, and their gums turn pale. In severe cases, the urine may turn dark red or brown as the body excretes destroyed blood cells.

## Cats Are Especially Vulnerable

Cats are more sensitive to onions than dogs. Their red blood cells are naturally more fragile, and even small amounts of onion powder in broth or baby food can cause illness. Because cats are smaller than most dogs, a tiny exposure can represent a large dose relative to their body weight. Any suspected ingestion in a cat should be treated as an emergency.

## Hidden Sources in Human Food

Onions hide in many foods that owners share with pets. Chicken broth, beef broth, soup, gravy, salad dressing, pasta sauce, pizza, seasoned meats, and instant rice mixes often contain onion or garlic powder. Even a small amount of broth poured over kibble can be enough to poison a small dog or cat over time.

## Delayed Symptoms Can Be Misleading

A pet that eats onions may seem fine for a day or two. Then weakness, lethargy, vomiting, and dark urine appear. Because the link to the food is not obvious, owners sometimes delay seeking care. If you know or suspect your pet ate onions, call a veterinarian before symptoms start.

## Treatment and Recovery

There is no antidote for onion poisoning. Treatment focuses on stopping further absorption, providing intravenous fluids, and supporting red blood cell production. In severe anemia, a blood transfusion may be needed. With prompt care, many pets recover, but the process can take days to weeks.

## Safe Flavor Alternatives

If you want to add flavor to your pet's food, use small amounts of plain cooked pumpkin, carrots, green beans, or a little low-sodium broth that is confirmed onion-free and garlic-free. Always read labels carefully, and avoid any broth or seasoning mix that lists onion, garlic, or "natural flavors" without clarification.
