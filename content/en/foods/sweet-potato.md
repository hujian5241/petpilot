# Sweet Potato

Sweet potatoes are a nutritious, fiber-rich vegetable that many dogs tolerate well. They are commonly used in commercial dog foods and treats as a carbohydrate source. Cooking is essential, as raw sweet potato is difficult to digest and can cause intestinal blockage.

For cats, sweet potato should be an occasional treat only. Avoid sweet potato dishes with added sugar, marshmallows, nutmeg, butter, or spices. Plain baked, steamed, or mashed sweet potato is best. Monitor your pet for loose stool when first introducing it.

## Why Sweet Potato Matters for Pets

Sweet potatoes appear in everything from premium kibble to homemade dog treat recipes. Their natural sweetness and bright color make them appealing to pet owners who want to add nutrition to meals. Because they are calorie-dense, portion control matters. Feeding plain cooked sweet potato instead of seasoned human dishes keeps the snack healthy and avoids common additives that can upset a pet's stomach or worse.

## How Sweet Potato Affects Dogs and Cats

When cooked, sweet potato becomes soft and digestible. Its fiber can help regulate bowel movements, while vitamins A, B6, and C support overall health. The high carbohydrate content means it can raise blood sugar, so diabetic pets should only receive small amounts under veterinary guidance. Raw sweet potato is firm and difficult to break down, which may lead to choking or an intestinal obstruction.

## Common Scenarios

- Preparing a homemade meal topper for a picky eater
  
- Baking sweet potato slices into chewy treats
  
- Wondering if a Thanksgiving casserole can be shared

- Feeding a cat a lick of mashed sweet potato
  
- Buying freeze-dried sweet potato cubes from a pet retailer

## What to Watch For

Most pets digest plain cooked sweet potato without problems. Watch for gas, bloating, vomiting, or diarrhea, especially after the first serving. If your pet ate raw sweet potato or a dish containing unsafe ingredients such as nutmeg, garlic, or onions, contact your veterinarian. Persistent vomiting, abdominal pain, or inability to pass stool can be signs of a blockage.

## Safer Alternatives

Carrots, pumpkin, and butternut squash offer similar fiber and vitamin benefits with less sugar. For a lower-calorie crunch, try cucumber slices or green beans. If you need a long-lasting chew, consider a vet-approved dental treat rather than large chunks of starchy vegetables.
