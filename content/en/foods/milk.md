# Milk

Despite the popular image of cats drinking milk, most adult cats and many dogs lose the ability to digest lactose. Drinking milk can lead to uncomfortable gas, bloating, and diarrhea. Small amounts of lactose-free pet milk are a safer alternative, but regular cow's milk is best avoided. If a pet accidentally laps a little, monitor for digestive upset.

## Why Milk Matters for Pets

Milk seems harmless because it is a staple in human diets, but it is not a natural food for adult dogs or cats. Many pet owners offer milk as a treat or comfort food without realizing that lactose intolerance is common. Repeated exposure can lead to chronic loose stools, dehydration in severe cases, and unnecessary calories that contribute to weight gain. Understanding which milk products are safe can help you choose better treats and avoid preventable tummy troubles.

## How Milk Affects Dogs and Cats

When a lactose-intolerant pet drinks milk, the undigested lactose moves into the large intestine and ferments. This fermentation produces gas and can pull extra water into the bowel, causing diarrhea. Some pets may also vomit or lose interest in food for a short time. The severity depends on the amount consumed and the individual animal's remaining lactase activity. Even pets that tolerate small amounts can develop sensitivity over time or after a larger serving.

## Common Scenarios

- **Accidental bowl lick.** A dog finishes the leftover cereal milk. A few tablespoons may be tolerated, but watch for soft stool or gas.
- **The classic saucer of milk.** A cat begs for milk, and an owner gives in. This often leads to diarrhea within hours.
- **Using milk to settle an upset stomach.** Milk can actually make digestive upset worse in lactose-intolerant pets. Water or a vet-recommended bland diet is safer.
- **Feeding a puppy or kitten cow's milk.** Young animals need species-specific milk replacers. Cow's milk lacks the right protein and fat balance and commonly causes diarrhea.
- **Flavored coffee or plant milks.** These may contain sweeteners like xylitol, which is highly toxic to dogs, or other ingredients that irritate the stomach.

## What to Watch For

Signs that milk did not agree with your pet include loose or watery stool, increased flatulence, abdominal bloating, vomiting, or decreased appetite. Most mild cases improve within a day once the milk passes through the digestive tract. If diarrhea is severe, contains blood, or lasts more than 24 hours, contact your veterinarian. Lethargy or repeated vomiting also deserves professional attention.

## Safer Alternatives

The best everyday drink for dogs and cats is fresh water. If you want to offer a creamy treat, choose a lactose-free milk designed specifically for pets. Small amounts of plain, unsweetened yogurt or cottage cheese may be better tolerated because fermentation reduces lactose, but these should still be introduced cautiously. For hydration support, cucumber slices or a small amount of low-sodium chicken broth can be good options.
