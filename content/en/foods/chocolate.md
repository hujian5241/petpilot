# Chocolate

Chocolate contains theobromine and caffeine, both of which are toxic to dogs and cats. Dark chocolate, baking chocolate, and cocoa powder contain the highest levels and are the most dangerous.

## Why Chocolate Is a Common Danger

Chocolate is found in almost every household and in many forms: candy bars, baked goods, hot cocoa, protein bars, and even some medications and supplements. Dogs are naturally attracted to its sweet smell and taste, which makes accidental poisoning common. The risk is highest during holidays when chocolate is plentiful and often left within reach.

## How Theobromine Affects Pets

Theobromine is a stimulant that pets cannot break down efficiently. It lingers in the body and acts on the central nervous system, heart, and muscles. Early signs include vomiting and diarrhea as the body tries to expel the toxin. As levels rise, pets become restless, pant heavily, and develop a rapid or irregular heartbeat. In severe cases, muscle tremors, seizures, and collapse can occur.

## Which Types Are Most Dangerous

Baking chocolate and cocoa powder contain the highest concentration of theobromine and can poison a dog with just a small amount. Dark chocolate is the next most dangerous. Milk chocolate contains less theobromine, but a large bar or a box of candies can still make a small dog seriously ill. White chocolate has almost no theobromine, but its high fat and sugar content can cause stomach upset or pancreatitis.

## Size Makes a Big Difference

A square of dark chocolate may cause only mild upset in a large Labrador but could be life-threatening for a Chihuahua. This is why veterinarians always calculate toxicity based on the pet's weight and the exact type and amount of chocolate eaten. Never assume a "small piece" is safe.

## Hidden Sources to Watch

Chocolate appears in many foods beyond candy. Brownies, chocolate chip cookies, chocolate cake, donuts, hot cocoa mix, protein powders, and some breakfast cereals all contain theobromine. Cocoa mulch used in gardens is another hazard that attracts dogs with its sweet smell.

## What Vets May Do

Treatment depends on timing and dose. If the chocolate was eaten recently, the vet may induce vomiting and give activated charcoal to reduce absorption. Intravenous fluids, heart monitoring, and medications to control tremors or seizures may be needed for larger ingestions. The sooner treatment begins, the better the outlook.

## Safer Treats

Instead of chocolate, offer small pieces of apple, banana, blueberries, or carrot. There are also commercial dog treats formulated to be safe and satisfying. Keep all chocolate, cocoa powder, and chocolate-containing baked goods stored well out of reach.
