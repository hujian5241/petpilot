# Eggs

Cooked eggs are a nutritious, easily digestible source of protein, vitamins, and minerals. They are often recommended for pets recovering from digestive upset.

Raw egg whites contain avidin, which can interfere with biotin absorption over time, and raw eggs may carry salmonella. Always cook eggs before feeding.

## Why Eggs Matter for Pets

Eggs are sometimes called a perfect protein because they contain all the essential amino acids in a highly digestible form. They also provide riboflavin, selenium, and other nutrients that support skin, coat, and muscle health. For pets with reduced appetites or recovering from illness, a plain cooked egg can be a gentle way to encourage eating. Because eggs are affordable and easy to prepare, they are a practical addition to a home-cooked treat rotation.

## How Eggs Affect Dogs and Cats

A cooked egg is soft on the stomach and quickly broken down into usable nutrients. The protein supports tissue repair, while the fat in the yolk provides energy. Raw eggs, on the other hand, introduce two main concerns: bacterial infection and biotin deficiency. Avidin in raw egg whites binds biotin, and over time this can lead to poor coat quality, skin problems, and other issues. Cooking solves both problems.

## Common Scenarios

- **Bland diet addition.** Boiled chicken and white rice is a classic combo, and a plain scrambled egg can also help settle a sensitive stomach.
- **Training treat.** A small piece of hard-boiled egg is a high-value reward for many dogs.
- **Backyard eggs.** Even fresh eggs from your own chickens can carry salmonella, so cooking is still important.
- **Seasoned breakfast scraps.** Butter, salt, garlic, onions, and hot sauce make eggs unsafe for pets.
- **Tempting a sick pet.** A warm, plainly cooked egg may encourage a cat or dog to eat when they are feeling off.
- **Raw diet inclusion.** Some raw feeders add raw eggs, but veterinary nutritionists generally advise against it because of the risks.

## What to Watch For

Most pets handle cooked eggs well, but watch for vomiting, diarrhea, or signs of an allergic reaction after the first few servings. Allergy signs can include itching, ear inflammation, or skin irritation. If your pet ate raw eggs, monitor for lethargy, fever, vomiting, or diarrhea, and contact your veterinarian. Chronic feeding of raw egg whites should be avoided because of the biotin concern.

## Safer Alternatives

If your pet cannot tolerate eggs, lean cooked meats like chicken breast or turkey are excellent protein sources. For pets needing a low-fat option, egg whites alone are lower in fat than whole eggs. Commercially prepared pet foods are already balanced, so treats and toppers should only make up a small portion of the overall diet. Always discuss major dietary changes with your veterinarian.
