# Spinach

Spinach contains beneficial vitamins and minerals, but it also contains oxalates, which can contribute to kidney stress and bladder stones in susceptible pets. For this reason, it should only be offered occasionally and in small quantities.

Cooking spinach reduces oxalate levels slightly and makes it easier to digest. Do not feed creamed spinach or dishes containing garlic, onions, or salt. Pets with a history of urinary stones should avoid spinach entirely.

## Why Spinach Matters for Pets

Spinach is often praised as a nutrient-rich green, and it can provide small amounts of vitamins A, C, and K. For healthy pets, a tiny portion now and then is generally acceptable. The key concern is its oxalate content, which can affect pets prone to urinary or kidney problems.

## How Spinach Affects Dogs and Cats

In small amounts, most dogs digest cooked spinach without issue. However, oxalates may interfere with mineral absorption and can contribute to calcium oxalate stone formation over time. Cats have little need for leafy greens, and those with urinary conditions should avoid spinach completely.

## Common Scenarios

- Adding a small spoonful of plain steamed spinach to a meal.
- A pet licks spinach dip containing garlic and cream.
- A dog with a history of bladder stones is offered leafy greens.
- A curious cat chews a raw spinach leaf from a salad.

## What to Watch For

Watch for signs of urinary discomfort, such as straining to urinate, blood in the urine, or frequent attempts to urinate. These may indicate an underlying issue rather than a one-time spinach treat. If you notice these signs, contact your vet promptly.

## Safer Alternatives

Green beans, carrots, or zucchini are lower in oxalates and make safer vegetable choices for most pets. They can be steamed plain and served in small portions.
